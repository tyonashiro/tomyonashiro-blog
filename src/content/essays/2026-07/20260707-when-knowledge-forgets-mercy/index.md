# When Knowledge Forgets Mercy

2026-07-07

![](hermes-rivera-aK6WGqxyHFw-unsplash.jpg)

## Three Encounters with Knowledge Turned Cold

I recently came across a Japanese business media [article](https://toyokeizai.net/articles/-/221131) about reunions. Its argument was simple and provocative: people over a certain age should avoid attending old school reunions. The article was not merely saying that reunions can be tiring, repetitive, or emotionally complicated. It seemed to suggest something colder: that such gatherings often trap people in illness, old memories, envy, resentment, and useless talk.

At one level, I understood the point. Reunions can become narrow. People may talk about medicine, illness, death, old classmates, past achievements, and old grievances. Some may compare lives in quiet or obvious ways. Some may feel bitter when they see how differently time has treated others. A reunion can reopen wounds that people thought had disappeared. It can also become a place where the past is repeated because the future feels less available.

Still, something in the article disturbed me. It was not the observation itself, but the posture behind it. The author seemed to look at these people from a privileged distance, as though he had already seen through them. The conversation of aging people was treated almost as evidence of decline. Their anger, envy, nostalgia, and smallness appeared to be things one should avoid in order to remain forward looking and alive.

That may sound like insight, but it may also be a refined form of contempt.

Later, I encountered another article that made a similar claim from a different angle. This one discussed [older people who easily become angry](https://news.livedoor.com/article/detail/31188422/). The argument seemed to be that people become angry because they feel useless, especially in a society where technology and innovation reduce the value of age, experience, and accumulated skill. Again, there was a partial truth in the claim. People do suffer when they feel unnecessary. A person who once had a role, authority, or social recognition may become defensive when those things disappear.

But the argument felt overgeneralized. Anger does not come from only one source. It may come from fear, humiliation, illness, loneliness, grief, financial insecurity, family wounds, social invisibility, or the pain of watching one’s world disappear. To explain anger simply as the reaction of someone who feels useless may be efficient, but it is too thin. It may diagnose something real, yet fail to see the whole person.

Then a third encounter gave clearer shape to my concern. I came across a new [book](https://amzn.asia/d/0gc2TZFl) whose title suggested that liberal arts or humanistic knowledge can become a “weapon.” The book itself may be more careful than its title. It may simply mean that history, philosophy, and human knowledge can help business people make better decisions in an uncertain age. In that sense, the phrase may be a marketing device, designed to tell practical readers that the liberal arts are not decorative or useless.

Even so, the language troubled me. What does it mean to turn humanistic knowledge into a weapon? History, philosophy, literature, religion, and the human sciences are not merely tools for competitive advantage. At their best, they do not teach us to stand above others. They teach us to see human beings more patiently, including ourselves. They reveal pride, weakness, mortality, desire, memory, fear, and the recurring illusions of the human heart.

Only after placing these three encounters side by side did I begin to see the deeper issue. The reunion article, the article about anger in older people, and the phrase about weaponizing liberal arts knowledge all seemed to point toward the same danger. Knowledge can become cold. It can make a person sharp without making that person wise. It can allow someone to explain human weakness while remaining untouched by it.

That is not a failure of intelligence. It is a failure of mercy.

## What a Reunion Actually Reveals

A reunion is not a leadership seminar. It is not a strategy meeting. It is not an intellectual salon. It is a strange social space where time returns in human form. People who once shared youth meet again after bodies have changed, careers have risen or fallen, parents have died, children have grown, marriages have succeeded or failed, and private sorrows have gathered quietly behind ordinary faces.

Naturally, the conversation may not be elevated. People talk about illness because illness has become real. They talk about medicine because the body has become less obedient. They talk about death because friends have begun to disappear. They talk about the past because the past may now feel more vivid than the future. They compare lives because human beings almost never escape comparison completely.

This is not always noble. Some of it may be petty. Some of it may be tiring. Some of it may be painful to hear. But we should be careful before calling it shallow.

A person speaking about medicine may be speaking, in the only language available at that moment, about dependence and fear. A person repeating an old story may be asking to be remembered as someone who once mattered. A person complaining about others may be revealing a wound that never healed. A person making a jealous remark may be showing the burden of a life that did not become what he or she hoped.

We do not need to excuse every unpleasant behavior. Compassion is not the same as approval. There are older people who are cruel, bitter, manipulative, or self pitying. Age does not automatically purify the soul. But neither does their weakness give us permission to view them as spiritually or intellectually inferior.

A reunion shows us something uncomfortable. It shows us that human life does not always mature cleanly. People do not simply become wise because they have lived long. Many remain bound to the same injuries, vanities, and anxieties they carried in youth. Some become smaller with age. Some become softer. Some become more generous. Some become more afraid. Aging reveals the person, but it also pressures the person. It compresses memory, loss, pride, regret, and fear into a narrower space.

If we look at this only with contempt, we miss the most important fact. These people are not simply “them.” They are possible versions of ourselves.

One day we may also speak too much about our illnesses. We may also repeat stories that younger people find boring. We may also feel ignored by a world that no longer needs our skills. We may also envy those who seem to have received what we lacked. We may also become angry, not because we are foolish, but because something in us still wants to matter.

To recognize this is not weakness. It is honesty.

The reunion, then, is not merely a place where old people reveal their decline. It is a moral test for those who observe them. Can we see weakness without immediately turning it into superiority? Can we listen to small talk without becoming small in our judgment of it? Can we remain awake to human limitation without becoming cold?

These questions matter because they reveal whether our knowledge has become wisdom or merely another instrument of separation.

## The Thinness of Useful Explanations

The article about older people who easily become angry raises a related issue. It points to something real. In a society transformed by technology, experience does not always carry the same value it once did. Skills become outdated. Older forms of authority lose force. Younger people may learn tools, platforms, and habits faster than those who spent decades mastering older systems. A person who was once needed may suddenly feel bypassed.

This can wound the soul. To lose usefulness is not only to lose a function. It can feel like losing one’s place in the world. People often build identity around work, family role, expertise, social recognition, or the ability to help others. When these are weakened, anger may appear. The anger may be defensive. It may be a way of protecting dignity when dignity feels threatened.

So the claim is not entirely wrong. People do become angry when they feel useless.

But the problem begins when a partial explanation becomes the whole explanation. Human anger is rarely so simple. It may come from feeling useless, but it may also come from humiliation, exhaustion, illness, loneliness, poverty, bereavement, fear of death, unresolved family pain, or the quiet terror of no longer understanding the world. Sometimes anger is pride. Sometimes it is grief that has not found a language. Sometimes it is fear wearing a louder face.

A useful explanation can become morally dangerous when it is too useful. It organizes reality quickly. It gives the reader a clean pattern. It allows us to say, “This is why those people are like that.” But the cleaner the explanation becomes, the more likely it is to erase the unevenness of actual human life.

This is especially tempting in business media. Readers are often busy. They want frameworks, patterns, models, and actionable insights. A sentence that simplifies human behavior can feel valuable because it gives the impression of mastery. It makes the messy human world easier to handle.

But people are not problems to be solved in the same way as market shifts, product cycles, or organizational charts. They are living beings whose motives are often mixed, hidden, and contradictory. Even when a pattern is real, the individual person remains more than the pattern.

This is why we should be cautious when someone explains anger, aging, envy, nostalgia, or resentment with too much neatness. The explanation may be partly correct, but it may still be spiritually insufficient. It may help us understand behavior while reducing our capacity to encounter the person behind it.

A theory that makes us less patient with people has already begun to corrupt our sight.

## When Shallow Opinions Become Common Sense

The troubling part is not only that one writer expressed a harsh view. Writers often exaggerate. Business media often rewards strong claims. Titles are made to attract attention, and arguments are sharpened so they can travel quickly. A provocative sentence can reach more readers than a careful paragraph.

The greater concern is what happens when this kind of opinion becomes accepted as normal wisdom.

A public figure who is known for knowledge, insight, and familiarity with innovation and the liberal arts carries a certain authority. When such a person writes that people become angry because they feel useless, many readers may receive it not as a limited observation but as a deep statement about human nature. The confidence of the formulation gives it force. The reputation of the author gives it weight. The platform gives it legitimacy.

Yet the statement remains shallow if it does not account for the full range of human suffering.

Modern public discourse often mistakes simplification for depth. A person who refuses sympathy can appear brave. A person who speaks coldly can appear honest. A person who diagnoses others without hesitation can appear intelligent. This is especially true in business culture, where clarity, speed, and decisiveness are often valued more than patience.

But human reality does not always yield to clean formulas. A formula can help us think, but it can also help us avoid seeing. Once we label people as resentful, outdated, useless, or trapped in the past, we no longer have to encounter them as persons. The label saves us the effort of compassion.

This is why shallow opinions are most dangerous when they are spoken by people who are widely regarded as deep thinkers. The audience may not notice the thinness because the surface is polished. The thought is delivered in the language of social analysis, innovation, history, or human science. It feels educated. It feels modern. It feels unsentimental.

But the absence of sentiment is not the same as wisdom. There is a kind of sentimentality that weakens judgment, but there is also a lack of feeling that damages judgment. To understand human beings, one must be able to think clearly and feel appropriately. If either side disappears, the understanding becomes distorted.

The normalization of cold intelligence is a serious matter. It trains readers to mistake distance for maturity. It teaches them that to be insightful is to be less affected by ordinary human weakness. It suggests that the person who can dismiss others has seen more deeply than the person who hesitates.

But hesitation may be the beginning of wisdom. The pause before judgment may contain more humanity than the clever diagnosis.

## Liberal Arts as Mirror, Not Weapon

This brings us to the wider concern: the recent language of using the liberal arts, history, philosophy, and human knowledge as a weapon. In one sense, the phrase is understandable. A book aimed at business readers needs a strong title. It wants to communicate that humanistic knowledge is not decorative. It is not a luxury for people outside practical life. It can shape judgment, decision making, leadership, and the ability to understand long term change.

There is truth in that. History helps us see patterns. Philosophy helps us examine assumptions. Literature helps us enter lives unlike our own. Religious thought helps us face suffering, finitude, guilt, hope, and the mystery of the person. Anthropology, sociology, and psychology help us understand institutions, rituals, identity, and conflict. The humanities and human sciences can protect us from the narrowness of the present.

The danger begins when this knowledge is framed mainly as an advantage.

A weapon is something we use to win, defeat, defend, or dominate. It places the learner in a posture of conflict. It suggests that knowledge is valuable because it strengthens our position against others. In the business world, this is easily translated into competitive advantage. The person who knows history can outthink others. The person who knows philosophy can see hidden structures. The person who understands human nature can influence, persuade, and lead.

Again, this is not entirely false. Knowledge does give power. Anyone who denies this is being naive. But the liberal arts are degraded when power becomes their primary meaning.

Humanistic knowledge should first be a mirror. Before it helps us interpret other people, it should reveal ourselves to ourselves. Before it sharpens our criticism of society, it should expose the disorder in our own souls. Before it gives us language for the weakness of others, it should make us aware of our own weakness.

This is one of the oldest purposes of education. To study humanity is not only to collect information about human beings. It is to be changed in one’s way of seeing. It is to become slower to judge, more attentive to suffering, more aware of pride, more sensitive to the tragic nature of life, and more capable of living with ambiguity.

If the liberal arts do not make us more humble, something has gone wrong. If history only teaches us how predictable people are, but not how fragile they are, we have not learned enough. If philosophy only gives us concepts with which to defeat others, but not the discipline to question ourselves, it has become vanity. If literature only makes us refined, but not more merciful, it has failed to reach the heart.

The weapon image is therefore not harmless. Words shape desire. If students and business people are told again and again that humanistic knowledge is a weapon, they may begin to approach it with the wrong appetite. They may read not to be corrected, but to be superior. They may study not to understand, but to possess an advantage. They may admire wisdom while secretly seeking status.

That is not education. It is self enhancement wearing the clothes of culture.

## The Hidden Pride of the Educated Person

Technical knowledge can make people arrogant. A person who knows programming, engineering, statistics, finance, or medicine may look down on those who do not. Expertise often creates distance. Specialized language can become a wall. Competence can become pride.

Yet humanistic knowledge carries a different danger. It does not merely say, “I know how to do something you cannot do.” It can quietly say, “I understand life more deeply than you do.”

That is a more seductive form of pride.

A person who studies history may begin to think he sees the cycles that ordinary people cannot see. A person who reads philosophy may believe she has escaped illusions that still govern others. A person who studies religion may begin to diagnose the spiritual poverty of those around him. A person who speaks about civilization, human nature, or social change may begin to stand above the people he claims to understand.

This is how knowledge becomes spiritually dangerous. It does not always make us openly arrogant. Sometimes it makes us gently condescending. We smile at people’s small talk. We grow impatient with their ordinary concerns. We believe we have moved beyond their anxieties. We call their pain predictable, their anger psychological, their nostalgia regressive, their faith naive, their hope sentimental.

The educated person may become blind in a refined way.

This blindness is difficult to correct because it hides behind intelligence. The person does not feel cruel. He feels perceptive. He does not feel proud. He feels awake. He does not believe he is dismissing others. He believes he is seeing them clearly.

But a person can know many things about humanity and still fail to love actual human beings. One can speak about tragedy and be impatient with a grieving person. One can speak about mortality and be annoyed by an old person’s fear of death. One can speak about social alienation and have no patience for the lonely. One can speak about history and feel contempt for those who are being crushed by it.

This is why spiritual pride is so dangerous. It is not merely the pride of wealth, talent, or status. It is the pride of believing that one has risen above ordinary human confusion. It can appear in religious people, intellectual people, activists, artists, executives, and scholars. It can appear anywhere knowledge becomes a source of identity rather than a path of conversion.

The cure is not anti intellectualism. We do not need less knowledge. We need knowledge that returns to humility. We need learning that makes us more aware of how easily we deceive ourselves. We need education that teaches us not only how to analyze the weakness of others, but how to recognize our own participation in that weakness.

The old religious word for this is repentance. In a broader sense, it means the willingness to turn the gaze back upon oneself. It means asking, “What in me wants to stand above these people? What in me enjoys seeing them as small? What fear in me is being protected by this judgment?”

Without that movement, the liberal arts become a polished path toward coldness.

## Knowledge That Learns to Be Gentle

The discussion of reunions and aging eventually returns us to a larger question about usefulness. Modern business culture often evaluates people according to adaptability, productivity, speed, innovation, and market value. Within that framework, aging appears mainly as loss. The older person is slower, less flexible, less aligned with new technology, less attractive to institutions that want constant renewal.

There is some truth in this. Aging does involve decline. It can bring rigidity, fear of change, dependence on old achievements, and resistance to new ways of thinking. Some older people do become obstacles in organizations. Some use seniority to silence younger voices. Some confuse experience with wisdom. These realities should not be denied.

But usefulness is not the full measure of a person.

If we treat human value as identical with usefulness, then aging becomes a humiliation from which no one can escape. The child is not yet useful. The sick person is temporarily less useful. The disabled person may be judged by a narrow standard of function. The elderly person may be viewed as a burden. A society that worships usefulness will eventually become cruel, even if it speaks the language of efficiency.

Human beings need to contribute, but they are not valuable only because they contribute. Their worth is not created by the market, the organization, or the speed of technological change. A person may lose status and still retain dignity. A person may become dependent and still deserve reverence. A person may speak slowly, repeat stories, and complain about illness, and still carry the mystery of a life that cannot be reduced to output.

Aging can make people smaller, but it can also make them deeper. It can bring gratitude, tenderness, humor, detachment, and the ability to distinguish what matters from what merely shines. It can teach people to forgive. It can teach them to stop performing. It can reveal the poverty of ambition and the grace of ordinary presence.

Of course, this does not happen automatically. Age alone does not sanctify anyone. An old fool is still a fool, and sometimes age intensifies foolishness. But the possibility of maturity remains real. To dismiss aging people because some have become bitter is to ignore the slow work by which some become wise.

The challenge is to hold two truths together. We should not romanticize age. We should not despise it either. We should not allow seniority to become tyranny. We should not allow youth, speed, and usefulness to become idols. We need discernment joined with compassion.

That same balance should guide our understanding of knowledge. The liberal arts should not train us to stand above ordinary life. They should train us to enter it more truthfully. They should teach us that human beings are proud and wounded, comic and tragic, petty and noble, foolish and beloved. They should help us see that the person complaining at the reunion is not merely a symbol of decline. He may also be a person afraid of disappearing. She may be a person trying to gather the fragments of a life into a story that still makes sense.

To see this does not mean we must remain in every unhealthy relationship. We may need distance. We may need silence. We may need to choose friendships that give life rather than drain it. Mercy is not the same as emotional surrender. But even when we step away, we can step away without contempt.

That difference matters.

There is a way of distancing ourselves that protects life. There is also a way of distancing ourselves that feeds pride. The first says, “I cannot remain here without losing something necessary.” The second says, “I am above these people.” The outward action may look similar, but the inner movement is different.

Humanistic knowledge should help us detect that difference. It should make us suspicious of our own superiority. It should remind us that the weaknesses we observe in others may already be present in us, perhaps in a more refined form. The old person’s resentment may look crude, but our contempt may be another version of the same wound. Their need to matter may appear obvious, but our need to be insightful may be no less needy.

This is why the language of weapons must be handled with care. There are times when knowledge must resist falsehood, injustice, manipulation, and cruelty. In that sense, knowledge can defend what is true and good. But if the primary image of knowledge is a weapon, we may forget that the first enemy is not always outside us. Often it is pride, fear, vanity, and the desire to be above others.

The highest use of the liberal arts is not to help us win conversations, defeat competitors, or diagnose the foolishness of the aging. It is to help us become more human. That means more truthful, but also more patient. More perceptive, but also more merciful. More aware of patterns, but also more attentive to persons.

Perhaps the real test of humanistic knowledge is simple. After reading history, philosophy, literature, and religion, can we look at an aging person speaking about illness and still listen with respect? Can we hear a jealous remark and recognize the pain beneath it? Can we notice the shallowness of a conversation without becoming shallow in our response? Can we see the human condition not as something beneath us, but as something we share?

If knowledge becomes only a weapon, it may help us defeat others. If it becomes wisdom, it may save us from the colder victory of becoming proud, distant, and alone.
