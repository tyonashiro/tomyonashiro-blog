# The Corruption of Good Intentions

2026-07-12

![](ink-swirling-in-water-on-wood.jpg)

## The Dream That Inspired the World

Many years ago, I encountered the Millennium Development Goals through Jeffrey Sachs’s book *The End of Poverty*. The idea was astonishing in its simplicity and moral scale. Humanity had reached a point where it could identify several of its deepest forms of suffering and agree that they should no longer be accepted as inevitable. Extreme poverty, hunger, child mortality, lack of education, preventable disease, and the unequal treatment of women were presented not as permanent features of civilization, but as problems that could be reduced through knowledge, resources, cooperation, and political will.

The optimism behind that vision was difficult to resist. It suggested that history did not have to remain a record of repeated resignation. Nations could set aside some of their differences and direct their attention toward measurable improvements in human life. The goals were ambitious, but they were not merely abstract declarations. They came with targets, timelines, institutions, and international commitments. They appeared to combine moral seriousness with practical planning.

At the time, I was deeply impressed by this possibility. Perhaps I also took it for granted. Once a goal enters the vocabulary of governments and international organizations, it begins to feel like part of the normal structure of the world. We hear the language repeatedly, see the logos, attend conferences, read reports, and observe companies attaching themselves to the cause. The original astonishment gradually fades. The goal becomes familiar, and familiarity makes it easier to overlook what happens after the declaration.

The Millennium Development Goals produced significant progress, although they were not fully achieved. They were later followed by the Sustainable Development Goals, which broadened the agenda and extended the timeline toward 2030. The transition was understandable. The earlier work was unfinished, and new concerns had become impossible to ignore. Poverty could not be separated from climate, health, inequality, education, institutions, and the use of natural resources.

Yet the expansion also raised another question, one that applies far beyond the MDGs or SDGs. What happens when a noble purpose enters an institution? What happens when moral language becomes a budget category, a professional field, a reporting requirement, or a business opportunity?

Every important social goal eventually requires organization. Good intentions alone do not build schools, preserve forests, conduct research, or reduce disease. They need money, expertise, regulation, measurement, and people willing to carry the work forward. Institutions are therefore necessary. Without them, moral aspiration often remains only aspiration.

But institutions do not merely preserve intentions. They also transform them.

## When Good Intentions Become Systems

The moment a purpose becomes institutional, new motivations begin to gather around it. Budgets must be secured, programs designed, staff hired, progress measured, and reports produced. Experts define standards, while governments, companies, universities, and nonprofit organizations compete for influence and resources.

None of these activities is necessarily corrupt. Most are unavoidable. A global goal cannot operate without administration, and administration cannot function without procedures. Even the most sincere institution needs salaries, procurement, schedules, committees, and systems of accountability.

The difficulty arises because the structure created to serve the goal develops interests of its own. A program established to reduce poverty may later become preoccupied with preserving its annual budget. A research center created to investigate a serious social problem may gradually focus on maintaining its grants, staff, and institutional status. An environmental initiative may begin with the protection of ecosystems but eventually organize itself around installation targets, certification processes, and subsidy distribution.

The original purpose does not disappear from official language. It may, in fact, be repeated more frequently than ever. Speeches, reports, and corporate materials continue to invoke poverty reduction, sustainability, inclusion, culture, innovation, and the public good. The words remain visible while the center of institutional attention quietly shifts.

This shift is rarely caused by deliberate dishonesty. Most people involved may still believe they are serving the original purpose. Yet they are also responding to incentives. Careers, budgets, reputations, and organizational survival depend on satisfying systems that are easier to measure than the purpose itself.

A government may decide that electric vehicles should be encouraged because they can contribute to lower emissions. Subsidies are introduced, production targets are announced, and private investment follows. Some companies bring useful technology and long-term commitment. Others enter because the available money makes the field attractive. The policy creates not only environmental action but also an industry organized around access to public support.

The same pattern can be seen in renewable energy. Japan’s expansion of solar power brought important benefits, especially in the effort to diversify energy sources. Yet the incentives also encouraged rapid installation, including developments on forests, hillsides, and other sensitive areas. A program created in the name of environmental protection could contribute to deforestation, damaged landscapes, drainage problems, and greater disaster risk.

The contradiction is not simply that a policy failed. It may have succeeded according to the indicator it was designed to improve. Solar capacity increased, subsidies were distributed, and targets were met. The official measure moved in the correct direction, even as the local landscape was altered.

A system can therefore achieve its metric while betraying its purpose.

## The Quiet Substitution

Corruption is often imagined as a visible moral violation. We think of bribery, theft, fraud, or abuse of power. These forms are real, but there is another kind of corruption that is less dramatic and perhaps more common. It occurs when something continues to use the language of its original purpose after another purpose has taken its place.

The substitutions often follow a recognizable pattern:

- Education becomes credential production.
- Research becomes publication output.
- Culture becomes national branding.
- Leadership becomes reputation management.
- Sustainability becomes reporting.
- Consulting becomes framework delivery.
- Poverty reduction becomes program expansion.

No institution openly declares that it has stopped caring about truth, education, culture, or the environment. Instead, proxies become increasingly important because they are easier to count, compare, present, and defend.

This resembles the principle often associated with Goodhart’s law. Once a measure becomes a target, people begin optimizing for the measure rather than the underlying reality. The indicator may have been useful at first. It may even have represented the purpose reasonably well. But once funding, promotion, prestige, or survival depends on it, the relationship changes.

Universities provide a familiar example. Research quality is difficult to evaluate directly, so institutions rely on publication counts, journal rankings, citations, grant income, and other visible measures. These indicators can be useful, but they also shape behavior. Scholars learn which topics attract grants, which language satisfies reviewers, and which forms of writing are considered professionally acceptable.

Over time, a field may become technically sophisticated while losing contact with the living questions that first gave it meaning. Philosophy professors may devote entire careers to the history of philosophers without allowing philosophy to disturb their own lives. Ethical questions become objects of specialization rather than demands placed upon the person asking them.

The historical study of philosophy remains valuable. Without careful scholarship, major works would be misunderstood, oversimplified, or forgotten. Yet philosophy cannot survive only as the administration of its own history. It must remain connected to suffering, courage, responsibility, death, freedom, work, friendship, and the ordinary conflicts of human life.

Academic insulation can protect serious thought from commercial pressure. It can also create a closed world in which scholars mainly write for reviewers, committees, and other specialists. The writing becomes difficult not always because the subject demands difficulty, but because complexity functions as evidence of professional legitimacy. A paper may satisfy every formal requirement while giving the reader no sense that anything was truly at stake.

The same substitution appears in corporate leadership. Executives are expected to communicate vision, values, and commitment. Their LinkedIn posts are often polished, timely, and emotionally calibrated. Many are prepared by communications teams, revised by legal departments, and shaped to support the company’s reputation.

There is nothing inherently wrong with receiving help in communication. Leaders represent organizations, and careless language can have serious consequences. Yet when every expression is mediated, polished, and strategically approved, leadership itself may be replaced by leadership communication. The public encounters the image of conviction without knowing whether the conviction exists.

Consultancy offers another version. Consultants are hired to improve strategy, efficiency, transformation, or profitability. At their best, they bring external perspective and disciplined analysis. At their weakest, they produce elegant frameworks, fashionable vocabulary, and attractive slides while remaining detached from implementation.

The client is left to bear the consequences. The consultant can claim that the recommendations were sound, even if the organization lacked the conditions to carry them out. Responsibility is divided so effectively that no one fully owns the result. The report is delivered, the workshop is completed, and the model is presented. The language of transformation remains even when no transformation occurs.

Corruption, in this sense, is not always the entrance of something obviously evil. It is the gradual replacement of a living purpose by a manageable substitute.

## Between the Market and the Monastery

It is tempting to conclude that public institutions and protected professions inevitably become wasteful. Yet such a conclusion would ignore another truth. Some human activities cannot survive if they are exposed entirely to market competition.

When I enter a large national museum, a research institute, or a quiet library, I often feel a particular kind of peace. These places are not constantly asking to be sold. Their work is not justified only by immediate demand or financial return. They preserve objects, languages, records, and forms of knowledge that may not be commercially valuable but remain essential to civilization.

A manuscript may remain unread for decades before becoming important to a future scholar. A scientific experiment may produce no immediate application. An ethnological collection may preserve the memory of a community whose ways of life are disappearing. A library may maintain books that few people borrow but that should not be allowed to vanish.

The value of these institutions lies partly in their freedom from the urgency of the market. They can protect what is slow, obscure, fragile, and not yet useful. Their mission may feel sacred because they preserve the possibility that truth, memory, and understanding deserve support even when they do not generate profit.

Capitalism is powerful, but it is not a complete philosophy of value. A market price tells us what someone is willing to pay under certain conditions. It does not tell us everything worth preserving.

Protection from the market, however, creates its own danger. An institution that no longer needs to satisfy customers does not become free from competition. The competition simply changes form. Researchers compete for grants, professors for appointments and recognition, administrators for influence, museums for government budgets and visibility, and cultural organizations for official approval.

The marketplace disappears, but another economy takes its place, one based on prestige, access, professional networks, and institutional legitimacy.

A tenured professor is protected from many external pressures. This protection can make intellectual courage possible. A scholar may investigate unpopular questions without fear of immediate dismissal. Long-term research may continue even when it does not fit current fashion.

But security is only a condition. It does not determine how a person will use it. For one scholar, tenure becomes the foundation for serious and independent work. For another, it becomes insulation from accountability. The original purpose of protection remains, but the protected position may gradually become an end in itself.

The same ambiguity exists in art and culture. Subcultures often emerge under neglect. Their creators work with limited resources, outside official approval, driven by necessity, curiosity, or a desire to speak to a community ignored by the mainstream.

Once authorities recognize their influence, funding follows. National programs then try to reproduce the energy that first emerged spontaneously. Japan’s Cool Japan initiative offers a clear example. Anime, manga, games, design, cuisine, and fashion had already built global audiences before the government attempted to organize them as national strategy.

Culture created the idea of Cool Japan. The state later tried to use Cool Japan to create culture. The reversal of cause and effect is significant. Administrative systems are often good at identifying visible success. They are much less reliable at recreating the conditions from which originality first appeared.

France’s public support for cinema presents a more complicated case. Funding has preserved a film culture that might otherwise have been overwhelmed by commercial competition. It has supported directors, genres, and artistic traditions that mass markets may not sustain. At the same time, critics have long argued that subsidy systems can produce insularity, dependence, and films shaped more by institutional expectations than by artistic necessity or audience experience.

Neither the market nor public support is free from corruption. Markets can reward spectacle, repetition, manipulation, and whatever captures attention most efficiently. Protected institutions can reward conformity, procedural skill, obscurity, and the ability to satisfy committees.

The healthier question may not be whether an activity is public or private. It may be whether the people involved still face meaningful responsibility for the value of what they produce. A private monopoly can become as complacent as a public bureaucracy. A publicly funded researcher can remain more intellectually alive than an entrepreneur chasing the latest trend. Ownership alone does not determine vitality.

Human excellence seems to require both shelter and exposure. People need enough security to pursue work that may not succeed quickly. They also need enough resistance to remain responsible, attentive, and honest. Too much pressure destroys depth, while too little consequence weakens purpose.

## The Uncommissioned Life

If motivation is always mixed, how can we recognize genuine commitment?

We cannot look directly into another person’s heart. Public declarations reveal little on their own. A speech may be sincere, strategic, or both. A research paper may reflect years of devotion, professional necessity, or a careful calculation of what a journal will accept. A corporate post may express the leader’s true convictions, even when written by someone else, but it may also be only a polished performance.

Absolute certainty is impossible. Still, certain forms of evidence are more revealing than others. One of them is what a person continues to do outside formal reward:

- What does the scholar read when no publication is required?
- What does the executive write when there is no communications team?
- What does the artist create without a grant?
- What does the consultant keep thinking about after the contract ends?
- What does the environmental advocate practice in private life?

These activities do not prove purity. Personal writing can become a form of branding. Public intellectuals can cultivate an image of authenticity as carefully as corporations cultivate reputation. Unpaid work can still produce status, followers, invitations, and influence.

Yet self-directed activity remains significant because it reveals what exceeds the formal role. A vocation often becomes visible in what a person does beyond what is required.

For me, writing is especially revealing. A personal essay creates a different kind of exposure from a corporate statement or academic paper. The writer must decide what he actually thinks, why the subject matters, and how much of himself he is willing to place behind the words.

Over time, a body of writing becomes a history of attention. It shows which questions repeatedly return, which concerns survive changes in fashion, and whether the person’s thinking deepens through experience. A single essay may be performance. Decades of sustained reflection are harder to dismiss.

This does not mean everyone must maintain a blog. Some of the most devoted people rarely write about themselves. A scientist may express commitment through patient laboratory work. An archivist may spend a lifetime preserving documents without publishing personal reflections. A teacher may reveal a vocation through years of attention to students. A craftsman may continue refining a skill long after public recognition has disappeared.

Authentic commitment usually leaves some uncommissioned trace. It may appear in writing, teaching, mentoring, volunteering, collecting, repairing, studying, caring for a place, or continuing a practice that no institution fully explains.

The uncommissioned life is not necessarily unpaid, nor does it need to be private. It refers to the part of a person’s activity that cannot be reduced to salary, promotion, approval, or institutional demand.

Institutions also need an uncommissioned life. A university committed to knowledge should preserve some fields even when they are not profitable or fashionable. A company that speaks of sustainability should make difficult operational changes that will never appear in advertising. A cultural organization should support creators who do not fit a national branding strategy. A development agency should listen to needs that do not match current funding categories.

When every activity can be explained by rankings, budgets, visibility, compliance, or growth, the stated mission may have become only a language of self-preservation. The uncommissioned part does not guarantee integrity, but its complete absence should make us attentive.

## Remembering Why We Began

It would be dangerous to judge people only by the purity of their motives. Human beings act from mixtures of duty, love, fear, ambition, habit, vanity, compassion, and self-interest. A professor may genuinely love a subject and still desire recognition. An executive may believe in a company’s mission while also protecting a career. An environmental entrepreneur may care about the planet and also want to become wealthy.

Mixed motives do not invalidate good work. A salary does not corrupt a vocation. A grant does not make research false. Public funding does not automatically destroy art. Professional communication does not prove that a leader has no convictions.

Corruption begins when the secondary motive quietly takes command while continuing to speak in the name of the original purpose. The central question is not whether self-interest exists, but whether the original commitment still governs it. When the two come into conflict, which one repeatedly wins?

Purity can become a destructive standard because it encourages moral surveillance. People begin searching for hidden motives in every action, convinced that the discovery of self-interest exposes hypocrisy. Since every human being has mixed motives, everyone becomes suspect.

Naivety creates the opposite failure. Noble language is accepted without examining incentives, consequences, or history. Institutions are trusted because their missions sound admirable. Leaders are believed because their statements are polished. Programs are defended because their goals are difficult to oppose.

A mature response requires neither suspicion nor innocence. It requires discernment.

Discernment does not assume corruption, but it does not refuse to recognize it. It looks at conduct over time, compares promises with consequences, and asks whether an institution can admit failure, revise its methods, and accept responsibility for unintended harm.

History becomes essential because isolated statements reveal very little. A single speech, report, grant, post, publication, or policy cannot establish integrity. Over time, however, patterns become visible. We see what a person continued doing after the subject stopped being fashionable. We see whether an organization protected its mission when doing so threatened its budget. We see whether a leader accepted responsibility after failure or merely changed the language.

The history of a person does not reveal complete inner purity. It shows continuity, contradiction, correction, sacrifice, and responsibility. Those are often better measures of integrity than declaration.

Perhaps the most useful question should not be directed first at institutions, governments, professors, consultants, or executives. It should be directed toward ourselves: Has the thing that now rewards me begun to replace the reason I started?

This question is not an accusation. It is a form of maintenance. Institutions need audits, but vocations also need them. A writer can become more concerned with attention than truth. A professor can become more concerned with publication than inquiry. A leader can become more concerned with image than responsibility. An activist can become more attached to the movement than to the people it was created to serve.

The change may occur gradually. The original words remain available, allowing us to believe that nothing fundamental has changed. Memory interrupts that substitution. It asks why the work began, what need first called it into existence, and whether the systems built to support it are still serving that need.

Memory does not return us to a perfectly innocent beginning. Such beginnings may never have existed. It simply restores the difference between the purpose and the structure created around it.

Good intentions will always be vulnerable when they enter the world. They must pass through institutions, budgets, professions, and human ambition. That vulnerability cannot be eliminated. Still, people and institutions can preserve the capacity to notice when the center has shifted. They can recover contact with consequences, maintain forms of activity not entirely explained by reward, and return repeatedly to the question that first gave the work meaning.

The survival of a vocation may depend less on perfect purity than on this willingness to remember.
